📖 The Story — Act II

The temptation & the forgetting

The moment Swami steps out with the coins, he sees his friends playing cricket. The thrill of the game completely overshadows his sense of duty — he joins in, and the short-term fun wipes the memory of his ailing grandmother from his mind.

The weight of guilt

As dusk falls and the game ends, Swami walks home alone. His tiredness turns into heavy, dark guilt. He suffers intense apprehension, imagining terrible scenarios:

He fears Granny is in agony because he delayed her cure · that she will brand him a sneak · that his parents will scold him harshly.

The anticlimax

When he returns, Granny is not furious — she is overwhelmed with relief that her grandson is home safely after dark. Her unconditional love only deepens Swami's remorse.

💡 A grandmother's worry for his safety overrides her own physical pain.
📒 Vocabulary

Key words

Sneak — a cowardly, dishonest or untrustworthy person.
Apprehension — anxiety or fear that something bad is about to happen.
Contrast — a striking difference seen when two things are placed side by side.

✍️ Writing Practice — answer on paper, then check
1. Why is the game of cricket a critical turning point in the plot?
▸ Show model answer
It is the inciting distraction that tests Swami's maturity, creating the central conflict between immediate pleasure (play) and moral responsibility (the errand).
2. Analyse Swami's thoughts on his walk home. What does this tell us about his conscience?
▸ Show model answer
He is full of dread, self-reproach and wild imagination — proof of a very active, healthy and loving conscience. He isn't cruel, just easily swayed by childish impulses.
3. Describe the ironic shift in Granny's attitude from when Swami left to when he returned.
▸ Show model answer
At first she focused on her own illness and wanted quick service; by the end her self-interest vanishes, replaced by pure, selfless anxiety for Swami's safety after dark.
4. How does R.K. Narayan show that Swami is not wicked, just a typical child?
▸ Show model answer
By focusing on Swami's intense internal guilt and regret — a genuinely bad child would not care about failing the errand or feel sick with remorse on the walk home.
5. What life lesson does the story teach?
▸ Show model answer
Neglecting small responsibilities for temporary pleasures can cause heavy emotional distress to ourselves and may harm those who depend on us.
