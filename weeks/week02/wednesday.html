🌍 Geography · Landforms of the Earth
Study these cards, then take the Quiz →
Earth's surface has many shapes called landforms — mountains, valleys, plateaus and plains. They are created by powerful forces acting from inside and outside the Earth.
⛰️ Mountains

What is a Mountain?

A natural elevation of Earth's surface with a distinct peak, higher than the surrounding land. Generally rises more than 600 m.

Fold Mountains

Formed when tectonic plates collide, crumpling the crust into folds. The most common type of mountain.

Examples: Himalayas (Asia) · Alps (Europe) · Andes (South America)

Block Mountains

Formed when the crust cracks along faults — a block is uplifted or the surrounding land sinks, leaving a raised block.

Examples: Vindhyas (India) · Black Forest (Germany) · Vosges (France)

Volcanic Mountains

Built from lava, ash and rocks ejected during volcanic eruptions over thousands of years.

Examples: Mt. Kilimanjaro (Africa) · Mt. Fujiyama (Japan)
🏔️ Valley

Rift Valley

The central block slips downward between two parallel faults, forming a long, low-lying depression in the earth's surface.

Examples: Rhine River Valley (Europe) · Narmada Valley (India)
🏜️ Plateau / Tableland

Plateau

An elevated, broad, flat area that rises sharply above the surrounding land. Plateaus are often rich in minerals.

Examples: Deccan Plateau (India) · Tibet Plateau · East African Plateau
🌾 Plains

Plains

Vast flat or gently rolling lowland. Formed by river sediment deposition over millions of years; extremely fertile land.

Examples: Gangetic Plains (India) · Great Plains (North America)
⚡ Forces That Shape the Earth

Endogenic Forces

Internal forces from tectonic plate movement that BUILD landforms from within the Earth.

Plate collision → Fold Mountains  ·  Faulting → Block Mountains

Exogenic Forces

External forces — rivers, wind, glaciers, erosion — that SHAPE and wear down existing landforms.

River deposition → Plains  ·  Erosion → Valleys
👨‍👩‍👧 People and Landforms

Life in Plains

High population density. Flat land = easy roads, railways and buildings. Fertile soil = farming and settlements.

Example: Gangetic Plains — most densely populated region in India

Life in Mountains

Low population density. Rugged terrain limits farming and infrastructure. Important for tourism and water supply.

Example: Himalayas — sparse population but key source of major rivers
